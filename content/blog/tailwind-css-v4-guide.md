When I started working on Saturday Club's brand, the instinct — as with most rebrands — was to update the visual identity first. New fonts, new colour palette, cleaner photography. But when we mapped their actual customer perception against where they wanted to position, it became clear that visual updates alone wouldn't move the needle.

The problem wasn't the look. It was the voice.

Saturday Club was trying to be two things at once: an elegant patisserie for gifting occasions and a casual neighbourhood café. The brand language reflected neither clearly. It was pleasant but forgettable — which in F&B is the same as invisible.

The repositioning started with the tone of voice. Before we touched a visual element, we agreed on a brand character: a little irreverent, genuinely warm, specific rather than generic. "Handcrafted with love" became "made on a Tuesday with an unreasonable amount of effort." The brand started to sound like someone.

Once the voice was clear, the visual choices became easier. The palette shifted toward warmer, more saturated tones. The typography choices leaned playful. The photography started featuring real moments rather than styled still lifes.

The lesson: brand identity work that starts with visuals and ends there rarely sticks. The voice has to come first. When customers can hear the brand before they see it, the visuals confirm rather than carry the entire load.
